# Daily Newsletter Digest - 2026-04-01

## Top Stories

### 1. What I Learned Onboarding Our AI Project Manager — *Every*
Every's consulting team built "Claudie," a Claude Code agent that saves 15 hours per week managing client project dashboards. Getting her working was harder than hiring a human: the team rebuilt her multiple times, learned that context window limits require routing raw data to local files rather than passing summaries between agents, and discovered that a mandatory handbook read at startup is the difference between reliable and erratic performance. Key insight: agents can only work with the context and tools you give them — so define the job precisely, architect for their cognitive constraints, and invest in documentation as seriously as you would for a new hire. Once stable, Claudie is being promoted to chief of staff with her own dedicated computer running 24/7.

### 2. Seven Things I've Learned Getting Companies to Use AI — *Every*
Every Consulting's head of tech consulting distils a year of enterprise AI adoption into a clear playbook: buy directly from the model provider (Claude/Gemini) rather than third-party wrappers, raise the ceiling for believers rather than mandating adoption for sceptics, run hands-on build workshops (at least 50% build time), assign genuinely "impossible" tasks that require AI to complete, make AI note-taking with an MCP connector mandatory for every meeting, map workflows into a spreadsheet and systematically automate each one, and shift training focus to "managing agents" — every individual contributor is now a manager with no management training. The consistent finding: these changes expand team throughput without cutting headcount.

### 3. What is Inference Engineering? — *The Pragmatic Engineer*
Philip Kiely (author of the new book "Inference Engineering") explains how inference — serving AI models in production — is becoming as important as training. With 2M+ open models on Hugging Face, every company building an AI product needs an inference strategy. The deepdive covers the full stack: runtime optimisations (batching, caching, quantisation, speculative decoding, parallelism, disaggregation), infrastructure (autoscaling, Kubernetes, multi-cloud), and tooling. Key stat: open models like Kimi K2 Thinking have briefly exceeded closed model capabilities, and switching from closed APIs to open models typically cuts costs by 80%+ at scale.

### 4. How to Evaluate AI Fluency in Technical Interviews — *Engineering Leadership*
Banning AI from technical interviews is outdated — over 50% of professional developers use it daily. The new hiring goal is evaluating *how* candidates use AI. Two red flags: "AI-avoidant" candidates who refuse to use it at all, and "AI-dependent" candidates who blindly accept suggestions. The Level 2/3 signal to hire for: using AI as a thought partner while maintaining critical oversight. Companies like Meta and Canva have already redesigned their process. The comparison is exact: we don't ban Google or Stack Overflow from interviews — we expect people to use those tools well.

### 5. How Roblox Uses AI to Translate 16 Languages in 100 Milliseconds — *ByteByteGo*
Roblox built a single Mixture-of-Experts model handling all 256 language-pair directions at 5,000+ chat translations per second with ~100ms latency. Rather than 256 separate models, routing activates specialised expert subnetworks per request. To hit production latency targets, the ~1B-parameter model was distilled to <650M parameters using a teacher-student approach, and the serving pipeline uses two independent caches (translation cache + encoder embedding cache) so the same Korean message needn't be re-encoded when translated to three different languages simultaneously. A custom reference-free quality estimation model scores translations without needing human "ground truth" — a pragmatic solution for 256 rare language pairs.

### 6. Stripe's AI Coding Agents Ship 1,300 PRs Per Week — *Lenny's Newsletter*
Stripe engineer Steve Kaliski reveals that Stripe's "minion" agents — triggered by a single Slack emoji — now ship ~1,300 pull requests per week. The key enabling factor wasn't the AI model; it was years of existing investment in developer experience (clear docs, blessed paths, comprehensive CI/CD). Cloud development environments that spin up in seconds are non-negotiable for running dozens of agents in parallel. The bottleneck has shifted from writing code to reviewing it, and eventually to generating enough good ideas. A bonus feature: AI agents that autonomously pay third-party services (Browser Base, Postal Form) open the door to ephemeral "hyper-useful single API" businesses optimised for agent consumers rather than human users.
