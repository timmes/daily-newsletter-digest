## Top Stories

### 1. Anthropic's Mythos Model Cements Industry Leadership — And Launches a Security Consortium
*Source: Newcomer*

Anthropic has released Mythos, its most powerful model to date, in a limited rollout to trusted security partners only — an accidental leak forced their hand. The model can systematically find and fix software vulnerabilities that have evaded detection for years, prompting Treasury Secretary Bessent and Fed Chair Powell to call an urgent meeting with bank executives about its implications. Anthropic appears set to leapfrog OpenAI in revenue run-rate (now surpassing $30B ARR), leaving many VCs kicking themselves for backing the wrong horse. In response to Mythos's capabilities, Anthropic launched **Project Glasswing**, an industry cybersecurity consortium that includes Google, Microsoft, and Amazon. Amazon's CSO described the dynamic well: "AI has up-leveled the skills of the bottom tier of attackers — but AI can really help the defenders." The HumanX conference consensus this week was that frontier model progress is moving faster than even the optimists expected, and Q1 VC deal volume shattered records on a broad basis (not just mega-deals).

### 2. Your Company's Data Is Worth More Than You Think
*Source: Every (Alex Duffy, Playtesting)*

Reddit, Shutterstock, and News Corp are each making hundreds of millions annually licensing proprietary data to AI labs, with contracts growing ~20% per year — and the market is just getting started. A small model fine-tuned on fewer than 2,000 examples from real lawyers and bankers recently beat all but the very best frontier models on corporate legal work, at a fraction of the cost, demonstrating that curated proprietary data beats raw scale for domain-specific tasks. The key insight: valuable data is high-quality AND keeps growing (new posts, new sessions, new decisions), and whoever controls the data that defines real-world expertise will shape which capabilities AI develops next. CMU and Stanford researchers found that AI benchmarks massively over-represent programming and math vs. the actual distribution of economic work — meaning most professions' valuable data has never even been measured. Companies can either license their data to labs or use it to fine-tune their own open-source models, building a flywheel moat; tools like Prime Intellect, Unsloth, and Tinker make this increasingly accessible. The teams paying attention now will decide what AI prioritizes — and that's a form of soft power over the future.

### 3. SiriusXM Encodes Assumptions into Code to Scale Platform Prioritization
*Source: Engineering Enablement*

SiriusXM's platform engineering team moved beyond RICE scoring to build a custom AI-assisted prioritization framework tailored to internal platform teams, who lack a direct revenue signal. They decomposed "impact" into weighted dimensions — developer speed, reliability, cost, security, and trust — and crucially, the act of assigning weights forces leadership to explicitly commit to what matters this quarter, making tradeoffs visible rather than hidden. The breakthrough insight is that most cross-team disagreements stem not from value differences but from hidden, conflicting, or stale assumptions — so SiriusXM built a central "assumptions repository" where user research and data decisions are stored, versioned, and reused. AI is used to surface and validate assumptions before they cause friction, and the shared repository means teams don't have to rediscover the same insights repeatedly. Eleanor Millman and Mina Tawadrous (the guests) noted this approach is shaping their 2026 roadmap and signals a broader shift toward builder-driven product development. Key takeaway: prioritization at scale is less about scoring correctly and more about making your assumptions visible and debatable.

### 4. Meta Launches Muse Spark — And Its Chatbot Has 16 Hidden Tools
*Source: Simon Willison's Newsletter*

Meta launched Muse Spark, its first new model since Llama 4 a year ago — hosted (not open weights), and currently in "private API preview." Self-reported benchmarks show it competitive with Opus 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and GPT 5.4, though it trails on Terminal-Bench 2.0, and Meta admits gaps in "long-horizon agentic systems and coding workflows." What's more interesting than the model itself is what Simon Willison extracted from the meta.ai chat interface: 16 tool capabilities including web browsing, a Code Interpreter (Python 3.9 sandbox with pandas/numpy/sklearn etc.), image generation, visual grounding (object detection/counting in images), sub-agent spawning, semantic search across Instagram/Threads/Facebook, and even third-party calendar integrations. The visual grounding tool appears to be a native model capability rather than Segment Anything — and the entire setup closely mirrors Claude Artifacts and ChatGPT Code Interpreter. Simon also commented on Anthropic's Project Glasswing: restricting Claude Mythos to security researchers "sounds necessary to me."

### 5. "Break Now, Fix Later" — A Framework for Understanding Trump's Policy Pattern
*Source: Prof G Media (Scott Galloway / Ed Elson)*

Ed Elson introduces BNFL ("Break Now, Fix Later") as the defining pattern of the Trump administration: a grand vision followed by destruction, then abandonment when building proves too hard. The White House ballroom is the literal metaphor — East Wing demolished, ballroom ruled unconstitutional, crater remains. The same pattern appears in Iran (12,000 airstrikes, gas prices up 30% US / 50% Europe, Ayatollah's son now in power and angrier), tariffs (Supreme Court ruled them unconstitutional, $160B must be repaid, supply chains still disrupted), and DOGE (200k workers fired, USAID eliminated; actual savings ~$215B vs the $2T promised, offset by the One Big Beautiful Bill adding $4.2T to national debt). The essay offers two interpretations: either Trump is simply in over his head (building is harder than breaking), or the destruction is deliberate. The contrast offered is Artemis II — a decade of planning, tens of thousands of collaborators, one iconic photo of Earth from space.
