### 1. The Green Boots Problem: Why Smart Leaders Keep Climbing Past the Point of No Return
**Source:** Fish Food for Thought (Apr 29)

The body known as "Green Boots" has been a macabre landmark on Everest's northeast ridge since 1996 — a cautionary marker that hundreds of climbers have passed on their way up, most of them returning safely, some of them not. Fish Food for Thought uses this image to frame a sharper question: why do intelligent, experienced leaders keep making the same catastrophic bets even when the evidence is screaming at them to stop? The essay identifies three failure modes — Summit Fever (letting the goal override present conditions), Oxygen Deprivation (decision-making collapses under extreme stress, precisely when it's needed most), and Solo Climbing (silencing internal dissent removes the last circuit breaker). WeWork ($47B valuation to zero), Theranos ($9B fraud), and FTX ($32B implosion) are examined through each lens. The positive counterexample is Satya Nadella killing Windows Phone in 2017 — a move that cost $7.6B in write-downs but freed Microsoft to generate $2T in market cap growth by not sunk-cost-climbing a dying peak. The essay's practical framework asks leaders to audit their current "mountains": which ones have visible Green Boots that the team has stopped seeing?

### 2. Compute Is the New Cash — Stripe's View of the Agent Economy
**Source:** Every — Context Window (Apr 29)

Dan Shipper interviews Emily Glassberg Sands, Head of Information at Stripe, and the resulting piece reframes how AI is changing money itself. The most striking finding: AI growth is net-new spending, not cannibalization of existing SaaS budgets — the top AI companies are reaching $30M ARR in 18 months, roughly three times faster than the best SaaS companies of 2018. Fraud has gone full-funnel: attackers no longer just steal credit cards, they steal compute and API tokens, running AI workloads on hijacked accounts before financial signals trip any alerts. Agents are already beginning to buy commodities autonomously — Stripe is seeing machine-initiated transactions that look nothing like human checkout flows. The most provocative claim: the AI subsidy era is ending. Labs have been burning investor capital to keep inference prices artificially low, and Glassberg Sands suggests June billing cycles may be the first signal that real economics are taking over. For anyone building on AI APIs, the window of cheap experimentation may be shorter than it looks.

### 3. The Genie Tarpit: AI-Generated Code Is Worse Than Human "Muddling"
**Source:** Kent Beck (Apr 29)

Kent Beck introduces a 2D quality space with working software on one axis and flexible code on the other, then places different development approaches on it. "Muddling" — the messy, improvised, barely-good-enough way most software actually gets written — scores mediocre on both axes but lands in a survivable zone. AI-generated code (what Beck calls "genie" output) scores worse than muddling on both dimensions simultaneously: it produces code that works less reliably and is harder to extend or maintain. Beck visualizes this as "down and to the left of muddling" — a distinct tarpit from which escape requires deliberate effort. The essay doesn't assign blame to the models; it notes that genies are trained on code written by humans to be read by humans, not maintained by agents. The solutions he tentatively gestures toward — better training data, better harnesses, more disciplined prompting — are all unsolved. The practical implication for engineering leaders is uncomfortable: a team that replaces muddling with AI generation may be trading a known problem for an invisible one.

### 4. Salesforce's Bet on Enterprise General Intelligence
**Source:** Engineering Leadership — Gregor Ojstersek (Apr 27)

Gregor Ojstersek reports from Salesforce TDX 2026, and the key takeaway is a deliberate philosophical distinction: EGI (Enterprise General Intelligence) rather than AGI. Where AGI is a research goal, EGI is a deployment strategy — AI purpose-built for enterprise context, compliance, and reliability, not raw capability. The most architecturally significant announcement is "Headless 360": Salesforce is refactoring everything (CRM, data, workflows) into API/MCP/CLI-first interfaces, explicitly so AI agents can interact with the entire platform without a browser. Agent training now uses simulation environments — synthetic enterprise scenarios for pre-deployment testing. Salesforce also released Agent Script, an open-source language for declarative agent behavior definition. For engineering leaders, the ADLC (AI Development Lifecycle) framework introduced for Claude Code is the most actionable piece: it proposes structured checkpoints for AI-assisted development analogous to the SDLC, addressing the quality and auditability gaps that make many enterprises hesitant about agentic coding tools.

### 5. The AI Adoption Bell Curve: Why Your Team Isn't One Group
**Source:** Level Up Newsletter #350 — Pat Kua (Apr 28)

Pat Kua applies the classic diffusion of innovation bell curve to AI adoption inside engineering teams and the result is immediately practical. The insight is simple but underused: innovators (2.5%), early adopters (13.5%), early majority (34%), late majority (34%), and laggards (16%) are not the same people, do not respond to the same arguments, and should not receive the same change management approach. Innovators need access and latitude; early adopters need social permission and visible examples; the early majority needs proof of safety and peer validation; the late majority needs mandates; laggards need removal of friction, not persuasion. Engineering leaders who treat AI adoption as a single communication problem will get the innovators and lose everyone else. The issue also covers ThoughtWorks Tech Radar Volume 34 and GitHub's use of eBPF for deployment safety — a technique that instruments kernel-level events to catch unsafe deployments before they affect production traffic.

## Trending

1. **AI code quality is not a solved problem** — Kent Beck's Genie Tarpit joins Engineering Enablement's longitudinal productivity study (Apr 29) in pushing back against the assumption that more AI = better code. The productivity measurement reality gap is widening: AI is fast, but fast code below the "muddling" baseline compounds into a quality debt that's hard to see until it hurts.

2. **The agent-native economy is acquiring real financial infrastructure** — Stripe's Emily Glassberg Sands (Every, Apr 29) and Salesforce's Headless 360 (Gregor, Apr 27) both describe the same shift from different angles: the enterprise software stack is being rebuilt so AI agents can transact and act without human intermediaries. The commerce layer is next.

3. **Leadership judgment remains the scarce resource** — Fish Food's Green Boots framework (Apr 29), Level Up's adoption bell curve (Apr 28), and Gregor's EGI positioning (Apr 27) all converge on the same point: the constraint is not AI capability, it's whether leaders can maintain clear-eyed judgment under conditions (Summit Fever, org pressure, adoption friction) designed to degrade it.
