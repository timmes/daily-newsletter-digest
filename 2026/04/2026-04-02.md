## Top Stories

### 1. What I Learned Onboarding Our AI Project Manager — *Every*
Every's consulting team built "Claudie," a Claude Code-based AI project manager that saves 15 hours per week by tracking all client project statuses across email, Google Docs, meeting transcripts, and calendars. Getting her up to speed proved harder than hiring a human: context window overflows caused subagents to summarise rather than pass raw data, silently degrading quality. The fix was having subagents dump raw data to local files rather than relay through the context window. Key lessons: define the job before deploying (agents only work with context and tools you give them), write a mandatory handbook the agent reads at every startup, and expand responsibilities only as trust is earned. Every now runs a "parallel org chart" of four humans and one AI employee, with two more AI hires in trial. The pattern of agents-as-colleagues is becoming a real workplace model.

### 2. What is Inference Engineering? — *The Pragmatic Engineer*
As open models proliferate (2M+ on Hugging Face, 25× more than five years ago), inference engineering is emerging from a niche into a core skill. Philip Kiely's book covers the three layers: Runtime (CUDA, PyTorch, vLLM — techniques include quantization, speculative decoding, KV caching, and prefill/decode disaggregation), Infrastructure (autoscaling, multi-cloud, GPU capacity), and Tooling. The gap between open and closed models effectively closed with DeepSeek V3/R1 in December 2024, making it viable for companies like Cursor, Clay, and Gamma to run in-house models at 80%+ lower cost and 4-nines uptime versus the 2-nines typical of commercial APIs. Investing in inference engineering makes most sense once your product has clear latency/cost constraints and usage patterns.

### 3. AI-Generated Merged Code Holds Steady at ~30% — *Engineering Enablement*
DX's Q1 2026 AI Impact Report preview: average AI-authored merged code rose from 22% to 27.4% quarter-over-quarter, but the increase is smaller than expected. Daily AI users saw the sharpest jump (24.1% → 30.8%), while weekly/monthly users barely moved — suggesting the ceiling isn't the model capability (which leapt in late 2025) but rather teams' workflows, habits, and trust. The stability around 30% likely reflects adaptation lag, not a model ceiling. Future reports will track this against telemetry-based metrics and explore whether higher AI-code ratios correlate with quality changes.

### 4. How Datadog Redefined Data Replication — *ByteByteGo*
Datadog's Metrics Summary page had 7-second p90 load times because Postgres was being asked to do search-engine work at scale. The solution: CDC (Change Data Capture) via Debezium reading Postgres's WAL, streaming changes into Kafka, with sink connectors pushing denormalized documents to Elasticsearch. Async replication was chosen over sync to avoid coupling write performance to downstream consumer health. Schema evolution is protected by a backward-compatible Kafka Schema Registry (Avro serialisation). The pipeline was automated end-to-end with Temporal workflows, scaling from one fix into a company-wide platform supporting Postgres→Postgres, Postgres→Iceberg, Cassandra, and cross-region Kafka. Key takeaway: identify whether your workload is OLTP or search/analytics before optimising — sometimes the right answer is a different database.

### 5. Ariane 5's "Reused Code" Catastrophe — *Fish Food for Thought*
Europe's Ariane 5 exploded 37 seconds after its 1996 maiden launch because of software inherited from Ariane 4 without re-validating its assumptions. Ariane 5's much higher horizontal velocity pushed a velocity value beyond the range a 16-bit integer could hold — a conversion the engineers had deliberately left unprotected because it "could never overflow" based on Ariane 4 flight history. Both redundant inertial units failed, and the flight computer misread diagnostic output as valid guidance, commanding fatal steering corrections. The lesson isn't just "test edge cases" — it's that reused, proven code carries hidden assumptions that become liabilities when context changes. For teams inheriting codebases or projects mid-flight: your job isn't just to execute the plan, it's to interrogate what you inherited.
