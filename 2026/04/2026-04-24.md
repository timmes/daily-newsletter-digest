### 1. The Pulse: AI Token Spending Out of Control — The Pragmatic Engineer

A 15-company deep dive reveals AI coding costs have become a boardroom problem. Some individual developers are spending $500/day on Claude Code alone, with overall token spend across teams rising 10–15x in under six months. The piece documents how the economics are forcing organizational policy changes: Anthropic and GitHub Copilot have both started throttling individual users to preserve capacity for enterprise accounts, a move that has sparked frustration among power users. Meta's developer morale is described as at an all-time low, partly attributed to AI tooling friction and unrealistic productivity expectations set by early benchmarks. The piece surfaces a structural tension that's becoming harder to ignore: the teams getting the most value from AI coding tools are also the ones racking up the highest bills, and finance teams are starting to push back. For engineering leaders, the practical takeaway is that token budgets — not headcount — may become the new constraint to manage.

### 2. GPT-5.5 Has It All — Every (Vibe Check)

Every's team put GPT-5.5 through their Senior Engineer Benchmark — a structured evaluation covering architecture, debugging, refactoring, and one-shot feature builds — and it scored 62.5 versus 33.5 for Anthropic's Opus 4.7. The reviewers describe GPT-5.5 as the best OpenAI writing model in over a year, with a particular edge in tasks involving structure and working within existing systems (brownfield rather than greenfield). For everyday, high-volume developer tasks it's fast and cost-effective. The one area where Opus 4.7 still holds an advantage: raw one-shot vibe coding on complex problems, where the Claude model's reasoning depth shows through. The benchmark results are significant not just for the numbers but for what they signal: the performance gap between frontier models has tightened to where workflow and UX integration — not raw capability — may increasingly determine which model teams default to.

### 3. Genie Lessons: Nobody Wants Agents — Kent Beck (Tidy First)

Kent Beck reports on field testing of Augment Code's Testing Intent system, a three-agent architecture (coordinator, implementer, verifier) designed to automate test generation at scale. The headline insight is deceptively simple: *nobody wants agents, they want outcomes*. Beck argues that multi-agent orchestration is a means to an end, not a feature to showcase — and that teams enamored with agent complexity often lose sight of whether the outcomes are actually improving. The piece documents real friction in the "manage-the-swarm" model: coordination overhead can eclipse the productivity gains when agent counts grow beyond what a human can meaningfully co-steer in real time. The frontier problem Beck identifies is multiplayer human co-steering — building systems where multiple humans can stay in the loop across a running multi-agent session without losing coherence. His framing reframes the benchmark debate: if you can't articulate the outcome you want, measuring the agent's performance is measuring the wrong thing.  
*Source: Kent Beck (Tidy First) | Date: 2026-04-23 | URL: https://tidyfirst.substack.com/p/genie-lessons*

### 4. Why We Suck at Asking Questions — Level Up Newsletter

Guest post by Jean Kang opens with a striking data point: children ask roughly 40,000 questions by age five — then largely stop. The culprit is the social training of school and work environments that implicitly reward answers over questions. Harvard research cited in the piece finds that question quality is among the strongest predictors of career advancement, yet it's almost never explicitly taught. Kang introduces a practical three-tier framework — Why → What If → How — as a scaffold for moving from surface-level clarification questions to the strategic and exploratory questions that build trust and unlock new information. The piece is aimed at engineers moving into leadership, where the ability to ask the right question in a room often matters more than having the right answer. In an AI-augmented workflow, the argument lands with extra weight: prompting is applied question-asking, and the engineers who prompt best are the ones who've already learned to ask questions that open rather than close.  
*Source: Level Up Newsletter | Date: 2026-04-23 | URL: https://levelup.patkua.com/p/why-we-suck-at-asking-questions*

### 5. Supercompanies, Webby Win, and Writing with AI — No Mercy/No Malice (Scott Galloway)

Galloway's newsletter opens with the announcement that No Mercy/No Malice has won the 2026 Webby Award — a data point he uses to pivot into a broader argument about the future of human writing in the AI era. The centerpiece of the issue is a preview of his "Supercompanies" livestream (April 28), examining the handful of companies — Apple, Nvidia, Amazon, Google, Meta — that have structurally separated themselves from the rest of the market. The most provocative section cites neuroscience research showing LLM-assisted authors display 55% less neural connectivity during writing compared to unassisted writing — a level the researchers compare to writing at twice the legal blood alcohol limit for driving. Galloway's read: AI writing assistance may be winning on output volume and polished surface quality while quietly eroding the cognitive depth that made distinctive writing distinctive. The piece echoes the Prof G Pod storytelling thesis from earlier this week while adding an empirical edge.
