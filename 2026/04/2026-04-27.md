### 1. Strait of Hormuz: The Slow-Motion Find-Out Phase — No Mercy No Malice

Two months after the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the second-order effects are beginning to emerge at scale. Scott Galloway's "Freedom of Navigation" essay traces the cascading consequences that markets have so far failed to price in. The helium crisis is particularly acute: 30% of global supply has been disrupted, spot prices have doubled, and the affected industries range from semiconductor fabs and AI data center cooling systems to the one-third of U.S. helium that cools MRI machines — putting AI infrastructure and healthcare in direct competition for a scarce resource. Fertilizer prices have surged (urea +65%, ammonia +40%), threatening to push 45 million people into acute hunger by midyear, with Zambia projected to experience 30% food-price inflation within a year. Consumer impacts have started materializing: condoms +30%, polyethylene +30¢/lb, USPS package surcharge +8%. The geopolitical precedent may be the most dangerous element: Iran is collecting passage tolls in crypto and yuan, and if the world normalizes toll-booth waterways, China's South China Sea territorial claims become the next logical test case.

### 2. Building a $0/API Local AI Lab: Qwen 3.6 on Two GPUs — The AI Architect

Tyler Folkman makes a compelling case that the decision to run a local LLM lab is not primarily about cost — it is about privacy, experimentation, and the freedom to break agents without thinking about usage meters. His setup: an RTX 4080 SUPER (16GB) plus an RTX 5060 Ti (16GB) running in parallel via CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=0,1, giving 32GB of VRAM and enough headroom to run 30B-class models with quantization. His daily driver is Qwen3.6-27B (SWE-bench Verified 77.2%), with Claude Opus 4.7 still the frontier leader at 87.6%. The stack — llama.cpp for inference, llama-swap for hot-swapping models via an OpenAI-compatible endpoint, and local agent configs that call localhost — means local models can become infrastructure, not just chat windows. Key config details: KV cache quantization (q8_0/q4_0) makes 131K context practical; Flash Attention and thinking mode enabled. The practical upshot: when agents loop 400 times locally, you kill the process rather than face an API bill; long-context experimentation at scale is possible without the meter running.

### 3. GPT-5.5, Model Wars, and the AI Sandwich — Every Context Window

Every's Sunday Context Window covers three interconnected threads from this week's AI frontier. First, GPT-5.5: Every's testing found it the strongest OpenAI writing model in about a year (62.5 vs Opus 4.7's 33.5 on the Senior Engineer Benchmark), fast enough for constant use, and particularly strong when working with an existing plan or system — though Opus 4.7 still edges it on one-shot vibe coding. Second, the "AI Sandwich" framework from Dan Shipper and Kieran Klaassen: humans now excel at the two ends — framing the problem before and judging the output after — while AI handles the middle (sustained execution); the human skill premium has migrated from implementation to taste, diagnosis, and multi-path thinking. Third, Monologue Notes: passive audio capture (calls, meetings, voice memos) exposed to coding agents via API, CLI, and MCP — turning conversations that already happen into queryable context without requiring intentional data entry.

### 4. If Agents Work 24/7, Why Pay Engineers 8/5? — Amazing CTO

Stephan Schmidt's weekly roundup covers four threads worth flagging. Async agents: the shift from synchronous, hand-held coding sessions to agents that run independently for hours is underway — the challenge is building backpressure systems, auto-ticket implementations, and isolated devcontainers. The Vercel security breach illustrates a new class of supply chain risk: one employee downloading game cheats on a work machine exposed the entire platform; separately, nine of twelve AI tools the author personally authorized had Google Workspace OAuth permissions to read all emails and access all Drive files. AI Agent Traps — a paper from SSRN — describes adversarial content embedded in webpages specifically designed to manipulate visiting agents; the lesson is that agents must have hard guards enforced from outside (devcontainers, isolated data pipelines) rather than relying on internal LLM judgment. Finally, the Anthropic tokenizer story: Opus 4.7 maps the same input to 1.0–1.35× more tokens than Opus 4.6 — a quiet price increase that compounds hard for teams running long context windows.
*Source: Amazing CTO (Stephan Schmidt) | Date: 2026-04-26 | URL: https://amazingcto.com*

### 5. SpaceX's $75B IPO: Faith in Elon as a Financial Instrument — Newcomer

The SpaceX IPO, expected in June, is shaping up as the defining stress test for the AI boom's capital markets thesis. The combined SpaceX/xAI entity lost $4.94 billion on $18.67 billion in 2025 revenue after heavy investment in xAI infrastructure; Starlink carries 60% of revenue at 60% EBITDA margins. Musk is seeking $75B at a $1.5–2T valuation — numbers that don't "remotely support" the valuation by normal metrics, but Musk's reality-distortion field has worked before. Unusually, 30% of IPO shares are allocated to retail investors (3× the norm), with roadshow tours of Starbase in Boca Chica beginning June 8. Dual-class share structure retains insider control. Elsewhere: Amazon invested a further $5B into Anthropic with an option to commit $20B more tied to AI infrastructure; DeepSeek raised outside capital for the first time; and Bill Gurley reminded the room that the entire AI boom is still heavily VC-subsidized — real market pricing hasn't arrived.

## Trending

1. **Geopolitical risk hitting AI infrastructure directly** — The Hormuz closure is no longer an abstract geopolitical event: the helium shortage threatens semiconductor fabs and AI data center cooling, while Iran collecting crypto tolls edges the global shipping system toward a fragmented model that would structurally raise costs for everyone building in the cloud. This is the first week the physical infrastructure thread has converged directly with the AI build-out story.

2. **Async AI agents as the new architecture** — Amazing CTO's "if agents work 24/7" and Every's Codex coverage point at the same shift: the interaction model is moving from human-in-the-loop to agents running in isolated devcontainers on long autonomous stretches, with humans reviewing output rather than steering execution. The design challenge is backpressure, isolation, and trust — not capability.

3. **Security as the next AI infrastructure crisis** — Vercel's supply-chain breach (via a game cheat download), AI Agent Traps adversarial content, and nine of twelve personal AI tools holding OAuth access to all email and Drive files: this week's newsletters document a widening attack surface that enterprises have yet to instrument. AI tools are accumulating permissions faster than security teams are auditing them.
