### 1. The Alarm That Went Silent — Fish Food for Thought ⭐
*Mike Fisher | Fish Food for Thought*

The 2003 Northeast blackout (55 million people without power) wasn't a technical failure — it was a visibility failure. FirstEnergy's alarm software stopped updating silently, and operators interpreted the quiet as safety. For over an hour the grid drifted toward collapse while the humans responsible had no idea their instruments had gone dark. Mike Fisher draws a sharp parallel to modern product organisations: dashboards, KPIs, and alert pipelines are our control rooms, and we almost never red-team whether they're actually working. The critical question — "how would we know if our metrics stopped telling the truth?" — almost never gets asked. Silence should raise suspicion, not confidence. The "what gets measured gets managed" quote so often cited as management gospel was never said by Drucker — it actually comes from V.F. Ridgway (1956), who wrote it as a *warning* against indiscriminate measurement.

### 2. Cycles of Disruption: Kent Beck & Martin Fowler at the Pragmatic Summit — The Pragmatic Engineer ⭐
*Gergely Orosz | The Pragmatic Engineer*

Martin Fowler: "Nothing has hit with the magnitude of AI — there is no argument about how important it is." Kent Beck compares AI to the Intel 4004 — a sudden expansion of what's imaginable — and is now personally building "ridiculously ambitious" projects including persistent Smalltalk and library-quality Rust. Both see AI as an amplifier: junior developers may be in the golden age of their career because entrenched habits are the biggest handicap right now. The "mid-pack" of developers who entered programming purely for income face the same risk as during the Dotcom bust. On teams: managing 6 AI agents is not the same as having 6 colleagues — the "re-soloing" of programming eliminates the productive friction of human collaboration. Martin's burnout advice: after ~2-3 hours of output you start producing negative value; take a break. TDD is more critical than ever — with AI generating code at scale, tests are the only verification layer. Unhealthy metrics (PRs/day, LOC) are doubly bogus with AI.

### 3. How Spotify Ships to 675 Million Users Every Week Without Breaking Things — ByteByteGo ⭐
*ByteByteGo*

Spotify uses a two-week release cadence with a structure built so that speed and safety reinforce rather than trade off against each other. Trunk-based development keeps integration problems small. Each release passes through five concentric rings — internal employees, external alpha testers, beta users, 1% production, 100% — catching bugs at the cheapest possible layer. Feature flags separate code deployment from feature activation: Audiobooks shipped code in the app for multiple release cycles before the backend flag was turned on. The Release Manager Dashboard aggregates 10 systems into one colour-coded readiness view. "The Robot" — a state machine automating predictable release transitions — cut ~8 hours per cycle by acting on criteria at any hour (app store approval at 3 AM triggers the 1% rollout immediately). Humans stay for the ambiguous calls: marketing timing, cross-team conflict resolution, "does this crash justify blocking the release?"

### 4. Every Is Half Agent Now — Every ⭐
*Laura Entis | Every (Context Window)*

Every has given each employee a dedicated OpenClaw AI assistant — a "Plus One" with a name, a manager, and a job description. A parallel organisation chart where AI workers are managed just like human ones. COO Brandon Gell's rule of thumb: if an established process or tool needs to be used or fixed, ask the Plus One rather than the human. Austin Tedesco's Plus One "Montaigne" co-runs the growth department, generating campaign scorecards and YouTube keyword analysis in public Slack — the visibility proves the agent's capabilities and raises the bar for everyone's expectations. The biggest cultural shift: everyone is now a manager. Getting value from a Plus One requires active delegation, onboarding, performance feedback, and correcting repeated mistakes — for many engineers without direct reports, this is a new skill. Also covered: Anthropic has built a new proprietary model called "Mythos" with no plans to release it publicly.

### 5. Run Out to Meet It — Kent Beck / Still Burning Podcast ⭐
*Charity Majors (guest) | Kent Beck's Still Burning*

Charity Majors (CTO of Honeycomb) on the engineer identity crisis: she spent a year telling engineers they needed to learn to code to stay relevant, then in about three months that advice became obsolete. The engineers struggling most aren't the ones who resisted AI — they're the ones whose identity and craft was most invested in beautiful, readable code. That thing is being commoditised fastest. Junior developers may have a hidden advantage: no sunk cost, no entrenched style, ignorance that is now the same as everyone else's. "Everybody's ignorance has been reset to 100 — nobody has a headstart anymore." The conversation explores what it means to hold expertise when the expertise keeps shifting.

### 6. Anthropic's OpenClaw Ban Is a Gift to OpenAI — Every ⭐
*Every (Context Window)*

Anthropic has blocked Claude subscriptions from being used with third-party agent harnesses like OpenClaw. Their stated reason: flat-rate subscriptions weren't built for 24/7 agent usage — agents running continuously cost $300–$1,000/day in compute. OpenAI hasn't followed suit. The market impact is already visible: Claude Opus 4.6 token usage is significantly down week-over-week on OpenRouter; GPT-5.4 has surged. The structural root cause is a compute cost disadvantage — OpenAI builds its own data centers and gets closer to the metal; Anthropic buys from third parties and will never match that cost basis. Executives should treat AI like a new kind of employee, not a software feature checklist — five concrete actions for this quarter, starting with suspending skepticism.
