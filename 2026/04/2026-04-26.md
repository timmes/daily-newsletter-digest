### 1. AI Token Spending Is Breaking Engineering Budgets — The Pragmatic Engineer

Gergely Orosz surveyed 15 tech companies and found AI token spend has exploded 10–15x in six months with no sign of decelerating, surfacing a tension between productivity gains and cost discipline that engineering leaders are only beginning to confront. At a late-stage fintech, some developers are now spending $500 per day on Claude Code alone — effectively doubling their employment cost. A healthcare company with a monthly token spend leaderboard reports "massive leverage" and says engineering is now blocked on Product and Design, a reversal that would have been unthinkable 18 months ago. GitHub Copilot and Anthropic are responding by throttling individual users to prioritize enterprise customers, while OpenAI is taking the opposite tack with Codex — a strategic split that signals diverging platform bets. The most honest framing came from a Series A founder: increase spend and start measuring, because killing AI momentum inside the company is probably worse than the cost. Separately, morale at Meta is described as hitting an all-time low, with developers furious about looming layoffs and an invasive employee tracking program rolled out across US offices.

### 2. Cognitive Debt: The Hidden Risk in AI-Driven Development — Engineering Enablement ⭐

Dr. Margaret-Anne Storey (co-author of the SPACE and DevEx frameworks) introduces a Triple Debt Model that reframes the risks of AI-accelerated development: technical debt (messy code), cognitive debt (erosion of shared team understanding), and intent debt (erosion of the externalized rationale that both humans and AI agents need to safely evolve a codebase). Technical debt lives in code; cognitive debt lives in people — and it's the harder one to see. A student team in her entrepreneurship course illustrates the pattern: they moved fast, shipped features, and by week eight could no longer explain why any design decision had been made; the shared theory of the system had quietly fragmented. Warning signs include team members hesitating to make changes out of fear, increasing reliance on one or two "tribal knowledge" holders, and a growing sense that the system is a black box. Practitioners responding to Storey's original posts describe mitigation strategies: requiring at least one human to fully understand each AI-generated change before it ships, writing tests that capture intent rather than just behavior, and treating prototypes as disposable. The core insight is blunt: velocity can outpace understanding, and when it does, the debt lives not in the repository but in the heads — and absence thereof — of the people responsible for the system.

### 3. Nobody Wants Agents — Kent Beck

In his fifth "Genie Lesson" from live AI coding sessions, Kent Beck arrives at a deceptively simple provocation: nobody actually wants agents or agent swarms — they want outcomes, and multi-agent is just a means to that end, one we keep confusing with the goal. Working with Intent by Augment Code (a coordinator-plus-implementer-plus-verifier system he dubs the "Freudian architecture: id, superego, ego") on an adaptive radix tree in Go, Beck found himself managing the swarm rather than the work — tracking which agent was doing what, deciding when to interrupt, holding state in his head that the system should have been holding for him. He points to the fundamental mismatch: what he wanted was "how much faster can we make this, how hard would it be, how much would it cost?" — outcomes, not orchestration. The frontier he identifies as genuinely unsolved is multiplayer augmented development: five agents can currently work on a codebase simultaneously, but five humans cannot, and reversing that asymmetry — building real-time collaborative AI-augmented development where multiple humans steer together — is "the real problem." His conclusion lands with characteristic economy: "I'm pretty sure it's not a coordinator with finger guns."
*Source: Software Design: Tidy First? (Kent Beck) | Date: 2026-04-23 | URL: https://tidyfirst.substack.com/p/genie-lessons-nobody-wants-agents*

### 4. What Will Be Scarce? The Starbucks Signal and the Post-AI Economy — Substack Weekender

Economist Alex Imas (Ghosts of Electricity) opens with a counterintuitive data point: Starbucks, after years of automating coffee-making to cut margins, has reversed course — CEO Brian Niccol found that handwritten notes on cups, ceramic mugs, and the return of comfortable seating drove customer satisfaction, and the company is now hiring more baristas and rolling back automation. This is the thesis in miniature: as AI makes the production of commodities cheap or free, scarcity shifts from the product to the human experience surrounding it. Imas draws on Marx's commodity form (industrialization severed products from their makers, enabling scale but erasing the human), Autor and Thompson's labor economics (whether AI raises or lowers wages depends on whether it automates the hard or easy parts of a job), and René Girard's mimetic theory of desire (people want what others want, and status goods are inherently social) to argue that human attention, craft, and presence will become the premium goods of the post-AI economy. The Armani suit, the bartender who knows your name, the barista writing a message on your cup — these are not inefficiencies to be optimized away but signals of a structural shift in what is scarce. The analysis connects directly to AI's trajectory in knowledge work: if machines handle expert tasks as well as routine ones, the economic value of human expertise doesn't disappear — it migrates to what machines cannot replicate, which is the irreducibly social, relational, and present.
*Source: Substack Weekender / Ghosts of Electricity (Alex Imas) | Date: 2026-04-25 | URL: https://aleximas.substack.com/p/what-will-be-scarce*

## Trending

1. **AI at enterprise scale is hitting financial and cognitive limits** — Pragmatic Engineer's survey of 15 companies documents token costs up 10–15x in six months; Engineering Enablement identifies cognitive debt as the invisible cost of AI velocity; Kent Beck experiences multi-agent coordination overhead in real time. The constraint has shifted from AI capability to sustainability — financial, cognitive, and organizational.

2. **The gap between AI output speed and human understanding** — Kent Beck and Dr. Storey reach the same conclusion from different vantage points: AI can produce faster than humans can comprehend or coordinate. Beck calls it outcome vs. feature confusion; Storey calls it cognitive and intent debt. The bottleneck is no longer writing code — it is maintaining a shared mental model of what the code does and why.

3. **Human presence as a post-automation premium** — The "What Will Be Scarce?" piece frames Starbucks' automation reversal as an early economic signal: as AI makes cognitive production cheap, human interaction and craft become scarce goods. This connects to the hospitality economy, the premium on relationships and status, and the deeper structural question of what remains irreplaceable once machines can do the rest.
