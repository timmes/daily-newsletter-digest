### 2. Cognitive Debt: The Hidden Risk in AI-Driven Software Development — Engineering Enablement

Dr. Margaret-Anne Storey (University of Victoria, co-author of the SPACE framework) introduces "cognitive debt" as the next major risk in AI-accelerated development — and argues it may be more dangerous than technical debt. Where technical debt accumulates in code, cognitive debt accumulates in people: it's the erosion of shared team understanding of *why* a system works the way it does. Storey's vivid example: a student startup team hit a wall at week 7 of a semester-long build. They could no longer make simple changes without breaking things — not because the code was bad, but because no one could explain the original design decisions. The shared "theory of the system" had fragmented. The extension to AI-augmented teams is direct: when AI generates large volumes of code quickly, the theory erodes faster than the team can rebuild it. Warning signs to watch for: hesitating to make changes for fear of unintended consequences, growing reliance on tribal knowledge, a rising sense that "the system is a black box." Mitigation requires deliberate practices — requiring at least one human to fully understand each AI-generated change before shipping, documenting *why* not just *what*, and running regular knowledge-sharing sessions. Storey's Triple Debt Model also introduces *intent debt* — when AI agents can't understand the rationale behind prior decisions, they can't safely extend the codebase either.

### 3. You're the Bread in the AI Sandwich — Every

Dan Shipper's *AI & I* podcast with Kieran Klaassen (GM of Every's Cora, creator of the "compound engineering" methodology) tackles a question that's becoming more urgent by the week: if AI handles execution, where do humans fit? Their answer is the sandwich metaphor — the model is the workhorse filling, and humans are the bread, providing framing and taste on either side. Klaassen's compound engineering framework breaks the engineering loop into four phases: Plan (human), Work (AI), Review (human), Compound. AI excels at the middle phase — sustained deep execution, running for "hours or days even now." What remains irreducibly human is diagnosing a problem from multiple angles (humans can generate many candidate solutions; agents tend to converge on one), and *taste* — the judgment of whether the output actually matches the vision in your head. That final layer of discernment, Shipper argues, is what separates creative work with a point of view from "generic slop." The episode extends their broader thesis: as AI lowers the execution floor, distinctly human judgment and aesthetic sense become the premium input.

### 4. Red-Teaming Your Strategy — Fish Food for Thought

Mike Fisher opens with a 2014 incident where an experienced Southwest Airlines crew landed on the wrong runway at night — not at Branson Airport but at a much smaller municipal airfield. The NTSB finding: once the pilots acquired a visually plausible runway, they stopped consulting their navigation instruments entirely. The checklist existed. The cross-check procedure existed. Neither survived the quiet pressure of a "routine" approach. Fisher applies this to product and leadership decisions: confidence is not the same as correctness, and confidence tends to close the verification loop prematurely. The piece delivers a seven-tool "Forced Cross-Check Toolkit" — practical mechanisms that introduce deliberate friction at decision points: (1) Disconfirming Evidence Check — ask "what would have to be true for this to be wrong?" and assign someone else to look for it; (2) Two-Runway Test — for ambiguous options, pre-define how you'll know which path you're actually on; (3) Time-Boxed Pre-Mortem — 15-20 minutes max, "it's six months later and this failed, why?"; (4) Kill Criteria Up Front — define the evidence that would make you stop; (5) Independent Verification — separate the advocate from the validator; (6) Assumption Ledger — write down confidence levels and evidence types for every key assumption; (7) Slow-Down-at-the-Brink Rule — verification should *increase* as you approach launch, not decrease. "Speed doesn't come from skipping checks. It comes from skipping surprises."

### 5. Why Storytelling Is Now the Most Valuable Skill in Tech — Prof G Pod

Scott Galloway's central provocation: AI was supposed to replace writing. Instead, it has made storytelling *more* valuable. As AI floods every channel with competent, generic content, genuine human perspective and narrative skill become scarce — and scarce things command premiums. Companies are now actively paying a premium for taste, point of view, and the ability to make a coherent argument that cuts through noise. The deep dive explores what this shift means for jobs, media, and brand value, and why the "AI writes everything now" narrative misses the structural dynamic: when generation is cheap, curation and perspective become the bottleneck. The same mechanism that made photography more valuable to artists (by eliminating the need to document literally) may be at work in knowledge work.

## Trending

1. **Cognitive debt as the new technical debt** — Both the Engineering Enablement piece and the Every "AI Sandwich" podcast converge on the same structural insight from different angles: AI is shifting the premium in software development from *execution* to *understanding*. The bottleneck is no longer writing code — it's maintaining the team's shared theory of why the system works. This theme is becoming the dominant through-line of the "limits of vibe coding / AI-augmented dev" conversation.

2. **AI lab consolidation and platform wars** — xAI's $60B Cursor acquisition structure is the clearest signal yet that the platform war for developer mindshare is entering a new, high-stakes phase. Anthropic (Claude Code), OpenAI (Codex), GitHub (Copilot), and now xAI/Cursor are all converging on the same layer. AI application companies that were building independently now face acquisition pressure from the labs themselves.

3. **Human premium in the AI era** — Three separate pieces today (AI Sandwich, Red-Teaming, Storytelling) address the same meta-question: what remains irreducibly human as AI automates execution? The emerging consensus: multi-path diagnosis, taste and aesthetic judgment, structured skepticism, and narrative perspective. These are not soft skills — they're the new hard skills.
