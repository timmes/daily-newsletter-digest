## Top Stories

### 1. CEOs Are Coding Again — And Claude Code's Source Code Leaked
*The Pragmatic Engineer*

Mark Zuckerberg has returned to writing code for the first time since 2006, quietly landing 3 diffs in Meta's monorepo this March using Claude Code. Y Combinator CEO Garry Tan has similarly resumed daily coding streaks since February, shipping 126 commits across 5 projects using his custom Claude Code setup "gstack." Both are starting on greenfield projects, avoiding the complexity of existing codebases. Meanwhile, Claude Code accidentally exposed its own source code via a leaked sourcemap file on March 31, revealing: an "anti-distillation" feature that injects fake tools to poison competitor training data; an unreleased autonomous agent mode called KAIROS with nightly memory distillation and GitHub webhook subscriptions; and a frustration-detection regex. The accidental leak highlights the irony of an AI company using regex (not LLMs) for sentiment analysis — and triggered a wave of DMCA takedowns, plus a legal grey-zone question: can code AI-ported to another language in a "clean room" be copyrighted?

### 2. Simon Willison: We've Crossed the AI Coding Inflection Point
*Simon Willison's Newsletter*

In a wide-ranging Lenny's Podcast appearance, Simon Willison argues that November 2025 marked a genuine inflection point — GPT-5.1 and Claude Opus 4.5/4.6 crossed a threshold from "mostly works" to "almost always does what you told it." This transforms coding agents from interesting to actually useful. Willison writes code on his phone walking the dog. He now produces ~95% of his code without typing it himself. The deeper shift: the bottleneck in software development has moved from implementation to testing and prototyping decisions. "A UI prototype is free now" — but evaluating three competing designs to pick the best remains unsolved. He also flags "dark factories" (StrongDM's experiment where nobody reads the code), and the mental exhaustion of managing 4 parallel agents by 11am. His advice: if vibe-coding for yourself, go wild; the moment you ship for others, stop and think.

### 3. How to Design for Human-Agent Interaction
*Every (Karri Saarinen, Linear CEO)*

The CEO of Linear argues that AI's "slippery feeling" is a design problem, not a model problem — and lays out six principles for designing agent-native products. Agents should: (1) always identify themselves as agents, never be mistaken for humans; (2) use the same platform patterns and actions as human users; (3) give instant but unobtrusive feedback when invoked; (4) be transparent about their internal state (thinking, waiting, executing, done); (5) stop immediately and permanently when asked to disengage; and (6) not be held directly accountable — the human who delegated the work owns the outcome. The key insight: accountability belongs to the designer of the system and the person who set the agent loose, not the agent itself. Linear now has agents as first-class users alongside humans, used by OpenAI and Coinbase to manage their own AI agents.

### 4. Scott Galloway: Five Chokepoints We Built Ourselves
*No Mercy / No Malice*

Galloway identifies five critical vulnerabilities the market created through short-termism. (1) Strait of Hormuz — Iran's asymmetric threats (drone swarms vs. $82M fighter jets) exposed US military overextension. (2) SpaceX — conducted 84% of US launches and owns 91% of LEO communications satellites; this week filed for a $75B IPO (the largest ever), with Musk seeking to become the world's first trillionaire while combining SpaceX with xAI. (3) Executive power — the Constitution's checks and balances have been systematically hollowed out since the 1930s, creating an "Article Two chokepoint." (4) Cloud — three companies (Amazon, Google, Microsoft) own two-thirds of the cloud market; a single AWS data center malfunction in Virginia took down Snapchat, Fortnite, Reddit, and 1,000+ others. (5) Taiwan/TSMC — one company controls 72% of the global chip foundry market; a blockade would cause an estimated 10% global GDP hit. "We didn't stumble into these chokepoints, we built them."

### 5. Mega-IPOs From SpaceX, OpenAI & Anthropic Will Test Retail Investor Faith
*Newcomer*

The prospective IPOs of SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic are coming in an unusual market condition: technically closed by historical logic (stocks down, unicorns still seeking exits), but powered by a new generation of retail investors conditioned by Tesla and Bitcoin to hold conviction through downturns. SpaceX's $75B IPO will reserve one-third of shares for retail investors — Musk betting they'll serve as anchor investors the way institutions traditionally would. OpenAI's latest $122B fundraise already included wealthy retail clients of investment banks. However, secondary market traders report OpenAI shares are now losing buyers as enthusiasm shifts to Anthropic, illustrating the volatile sentiment that could make or break these offerings. Separately: activist investor Irenic bought 2.5% of Snap and demanded Evan Spiegel spin out Spectacles — but admits it can only "attempt to persuade" due to Snap's voting structure.

### 6. Anthropic Quietly Acquires Coefficient Bio for $400M+ in Stock
*Newcomer*

Anthropic has purchased Coefficient Bio, a Dimension-backed stealth startup working on AI models for biological research, in a deal worth over $400 million in stock. The eight-month-old company was pursuing "artificial superintelligence for science" for the biopharma industry. Coefficient Bio's team joins Anthropic's Health Care Life Sciences division. Dimension, which co-owned half the startup, is boasting a 38,513% IRR on the investment. The deal stands in sharp contrast to OpenAI's concurrent media acquisition (the TBPN podcast), with Newcomer noting: "It's hard not to marvel at the contrast between Anthropic's science-forward acquisition and OpenAI's media-oriented purchase."
