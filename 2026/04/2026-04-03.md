## Top Stories

### 1. Anthropic Acquires Coefficient Bio for $400M+ in Stock
*Newcomer*

Anthropic has quietly purchased Coefficient Bio, a Dimension-backed stealth startup, in a deal worth just over $400 million in stock. Coefficient Bio — formally founded only eight months ago — was building AI models for biological research, with the goal of nothing less than artificial superintelligence for science. The team is joining Anthropic's Health Care Life Sciences division, led by Eric Kauderer-Abrams. Dimension, which co-owned half the startup, is boasting a 38,513% IRR on the investment. The deal stands in sharp contrast to OpenAI's media-oriented acquisition announced the same day, and extends Anthropic's pattern of acquiring technical infrastructure companies (previously Bun and Vercept). This signals Anthropic is moving beyond language models into applied science, with healthcare and biology as a key frontier.

### 2. Industry Leaders Are Returning to Hands-On Coding with AI
*The Pragmatic Engineer*

Gergely Orosz reports on a notable cultural shift: senior engineering leaders — VPs, CTOs, Directors — who previously stepped away from day-to-day coding are returning to hands-on work, enabled by AI tools like Claude Code and Cursor. The piece explores what this means for engineering culture, team expectations, and whether the traditional split between "management track" and "individual contributor track" is blurring. For teams, this has real implications: leaders who code again are faster to spot quality issues, more credible in technical discussions, and better at setting realistic timelines — but it also raises questions about manager bandwidth and whether coding is the best use of senior leadership time.

### 3. AI-Generated Merged Code Reaches 27.4% — But Daily Users Hit 30.8%
*Engineering Enablement*

DX's Q1 2026 AI Impact Report preview (500+ organisations surveyed): the average share of merged code attributed to AI rose from 22% (Q4 2025) to 27.4% — a modest increase that masks a more interesting story. Daily AI users are already at 30.8%, up from 24.1%. The stability in the overall number likely reflects most teams not yet adapting their workflows to take full advantage of the dramatic model capability improvements that arrived in late 2025 (Opus 4.5 and similar). The bottleneck has shifted from tool capability to habit change. The full Q1 report — which will also add telemetry-based code tracking alongside self-reported data — arrives later this month.

### 4. Should You Still Become an Engineering Manager in 2026?
*Engineering Leadership — Gregor Ojstersek*

Gregor revisits the EM vs IC question in an AI-changed landscape. Key shifts since 2020: IC and management salaries have converged (some ML IC roles now pay more than EMs); the IC path has been normalised at more companies; Amazon raised its manager-to-IC ratio by 15%, triggering industry-wide restructuring; and roles like EM, Staff Engineer, and Architect are increasingly expected to cover each other's ground. The "product engineer" archetype — owning both product thinking and engineering execution end-to-end — is growing fast at OpenAI, Anthropic, and Lovable. Middle management has gotten harder: managing unrealistic AI expectations from leadership is now a core EM skill. Gregor's answer to the titular question is paywalled, but his framing is clear: impact > title is the dominant framework now.

### 5. 15 Insider Negotiating Secrets for Higher Compensation
*Level Up Newsletter — Ethan Evans & Jason Yoong*

Advice from someone who managed 800+ person organisations at Amazon and reviewed hundreds of comp decisions. The core principle: compensation negotiation can move you to the top of your value band — but it can't substitute for building actual value first. Think of it like a powerlifter: build the muscle (value), then optimise the lift (negotiation). Key insights: impact that isn't *visible* won't be rewarded (one employee was underpaid because leadership didn't know what he did); triangulate your market value using benchmarking tools, peer conversations, and competing offers before any negotiation; your maximum leverage point is when receiving a new offer, where nothing is fixed and internal precedent doesn't apply. Most professionals over-index on negotiation tactics and under-invest in the underlying capabilities that justify higher pay.

### 6. How Datadog Turned a Single Database Problem Into a Company-Wide Platform
*ByteByteGo*

A detailed technical case study on how Datadog solved 7-second p90 latency on its Metrics Summary page (an 82K × 817K row join in Postgres) and turned the solution into a scalable data replication platform. The key insight: Postgres was doing the wrong job — OLTP databases shouldn't serve real-time search workloads. The fix was Change Data Capture (Debezium → Kafka → Elasticsearch): Postgres writes remain unchanged, but search queries now hit a purpose-built search engine. Async replication was chosen over synchronous for resilience and speed, accepting a small lag window. Schema evolution was protected by automated SQL migration validation + a Kafka Schema Registry with backward compatibility rules. The whole provisioning process was automated via Temporal. What started as fixing one page became Postgres→Iceberg pipelines, Cassandra replication, and cross-region Kafka — all on the same platform.
