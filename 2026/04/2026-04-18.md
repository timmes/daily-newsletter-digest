## Top Stories

### 1. How an AI-Native Startup From SF Works
*Engineering Leadership (Gregor Ojstersek) · Apr 20*

Gregor Ojstersek takes us inside Weave, a 10-engineer seed-stage startup in San Francisco that has gone fully AI-native in its development process. There are no mandatory human code reviews — instead, five AI review tools (Cursor Bugbot, GrepTile, Cubic, Wispbit, Continue) run in parallel on every pull request, with human review optional. The team uses Cursor and Claude Code for all development, Linear for lightweight project tracking, and caps planning horizons at one month. Their single mandatory meeting is a 9AM standup; everything else is async. The key cultural insight is that they don't treat AI reviewers as supplements to human judgment — they treat them as first-class team members and continuously evaluate their effectiveness. Feature flags replace big launches, and recurring issues get encoded back into the AI reviewers rather than written up in review docs no one reads.

### 2. The Folder Is the Agent
*Every / Source Code (Kieran Klaassen) · Apr 20*

After three months trying to build complex AI agent swarms, Kieran Klaassen discovered the answer was a folder. A project directory containing a CLAUDE.md, accumulated context, and specialized sub-agent instructions is all that's needed to turn a general-purpose model into a genuine domain expert — no fine-tuning, no complex orchestration. Klaassen now runs 44 such agents, each pointing at its own folder, connected by a Ruby dispatch layer that routes incoming work to the right agent while he sleeps. The CLAUDE.md is the encoding layer: business context, coding standards, past decisions, common patterns. The folder accumulates memory automatically through normal use. The practical lesson is architectural: instead of building one big agent that tries to know everything, build a thin routing layer over many small, deeply contextualised agents. Composability beats monoliths here as reliably as it does in microservices.

### 3. We Need to Talk About AI Autopilot
*Every (Katie Parrott) · Apr 20*

Katie Parrott names a problem most teams aren't talking about yet: AI automation complacency. The same cognitive mechanism that caused aviation autopilot accidents — processing fluency, the psychological effect that makes polished, confident output feel correct even when wrong — now applies to AI-generated code, copy, and analysis. A 2021 study found that forcing users to explain their reasoning before accepting an AI suggestion cut mistaken acceptances by roughly 50%. Parrott's three cognitive forcing functions: write your own rough position before reading the AI's output (think before you look); review AI suggestions the next day when fluency has faded (build in a gap); require a one-sentence justification before every acceptance (explain why you're accepting it). The article connects to Every's own "Proof" editor, which tracks the ratio of human to machine words as a complacency signal. For anyone shipping AI-assisted work at speed, this is the practical safety framework missing from most workflows.

### 4. KTLO Budget and the Absorption Capacity Problem
*Level Up #349 (Pat Kua) · Apr 19*

Pat Kua introduces the KTLO (Keep the Lights On) budget as a structural fix for teams that keep losing technical improvement time to feature pressure. The idea: agree upfront on a fixed 20–30% of engineering capacity reserved for tech improvements, without requiring case-by-case justification. It works best in high-trust environments where leadership genuinely understands compounding technical debt, and it gets crowded out most reliably by inexperienced leaders who say yes to everything. The issue also surfaces a more fundamental problem from Bence A. Tóth at Zendesk: as AI makes code generation nearly free, the real bottleneck shifts to "absorption capacity" — a team's ability to review, understand, and safely deploy the code that gets generated. The implication for engineering leaders is significant: the capacity constraint is no longer writing speed, it's comprehension and judgment speed. Managing AI agent output is structurally identical to managing engineers, which is why the issue's framing — "using agents is literally engineering management" — lands precisely.

### 5. I Tested Opus 4.7 Against Sonnet 4.6. The Newer Model Lost.
*The AI Architect (Tyler Folkman) · Apr 19*

Tyler Folkman ran a real-world benchmark — a disk-map TUI implementation in Rust — and got results that directly contradict SWE-bench predictions. Sonnet 4.6 scored 68/100; Opus 4.7 scored 63/100; GLM-5.1 came in at 58/100. SWE-bench had predicted Opus 4.7 would win by a wide margin (87.6% vs 79.6%). What went wrong with Opus 4.7: it produced a single-file 393-line implementation (itself a smell), skipped vim-style keybindings without asking, and failed to handle multi-byte escape sequences. Sonnet 4.6's implementation was more modular, included vim keys, and handled edge cases more thoughtfully. Folkman's lessons are worth internalising: single-file implementations signal the model isn't thinking about maintainability; circular symlink handling is universally broken across all models (always add a visited-set check); vim key support is a proxy for quality judgment on terminal tools. Most importantly: build your own domain benchmarks, because provider metrics optimise for what gets measured, not for what you actually build.

### 6. Why Are Markets Hitting Record Highs?
*Prof G Markets · Apr 20*

Three reasons the Dow hit all-time highs despite the Iran crisis: historical wars average only a 4% market decline before full recovery; this is the fifth consecutive quarter of double-digit earnings growth; and the US is meaningfully less oil-dependent than during previous Middle East escalations. The issue coins "the ketamine economy" — the market's growing dissociation from societal well-being — and points to a more unsettling data cluster: 44% of Gen Z workers surveyed admit to actively sabotaging AI rollouts at their companies; 48 data center projects representing $156B in planned investment have been blocked or stalled; bipartisan opposition is running at 55% Republican, 45% Democrat. An incendiary device was thrown at Sam Altman's house. The piece frames this not as isolated incidents but as the beginning of organized physical resistance to AI infrastructure — a shift from protest to sabotage that has no recent parallel in the tech industry.

## Trending

**1. Real-world AI model performance diverges sharply from benchmark predictions**
Two independent tests this week found Opus 4.7 underperforming Sonnet 4.6 on real implementation tasks (Folkman's Rust benchmark, Every's vibe check). The message is consistent: SWE-bench and similar leaderboards optimise for what gets measured, not for your specific work. Build your own benchmarks.

**2. The folder-as-agent architecture is becoming the dominant agentic pattern**
Kieran Klaassen (44 agents), Gregor Ojstersek (AI-native startup), and Level Up's curation all point to the same structure: a CLAUDE.md + project context per domain, routed by a thin dispatch layer. Complex orchestration is losing to simple contextualisation.

**3. AI displacement entering the physical resistance phase**
From Andrew Yang's capital-displaces-labor framing to Prof G's data on Gen Z sabotage and blocked data centers, the week's macro signal is that AI's economic disruption is beginning to generate organised, physical pushback — not just think-pieces.
