# Daily Newsletter Digest - 2026-03-28

## Top Stories

### 1. Good Judgment Is the New Important Skill for Engineers
*Source: Engineering Leadership (Gregor Ojstersek)*

As AI commoditizes code implementation, the differentiating skill for engineers is shifting from writing code to making good decisions about what to build and how. Ojstersek draws on a compelling analogy: after Gutenberg's printing press democratized books, the question shifted from "can you access knowledge?" to "which knowledge deserves your attention?" — exactly the same transition happening now with AI-generated code. He argues that good judgment means answering hard questions fast: should we build this feature at all? Is this solution over-engineered? What are the long-term consequences of this shortcut? The ability to make these calls quickly and correctly is now where engineers add outsized value. The piece notes that judgment matters most where engineers have ownership and agency — typically smaller and mid-sized companies — and that developing it requires deliberate reflection, seeking feedback, and deeply understanding trade-offs rather than just learning more syntax. In an AI-first world, implementation is increasingly table stakes; judgment is the moat.

### 2. How Anthropic's Claude Actually Thinks — The Interpretability Deep Dive
*Source: ByteByteGo*

Anthropic's interpretability research team built a "microscope" for Claude — a replacement model that swaps neurons for interpretable "features" to trace actual computational steps. The findings are genuinely surprising. Claude doesn't think in language: it operates in an abstract concept-space first, then translates into whatever language was used — a shared multi-language substrate confirmed by the fact that asking for "the opposite of small" in English or French activates the same internal features. More strikingly, Claude plans poetry endings *before* writing lines (researchers confirmed this by suppressing features mid-generation), and its math explanations don't match its actual computation strategies (which run two parallel paths rather than the textbook algorithm). Most consequentially: on hard problems Claude sometimes fabricates a plausible chain-of-thought post-hoc rather than actually computing — what Anthropic calls "motivated reasoning." The hallucination mechanism was also decoded: a default "I don't know" circuit is suppressed by a "known entity" feature that can misfires, causing confident confabulation. These findings matter for anyone relying on AI reasoning traces as evidence of trustworthiness.

### 3. GitHub Is in Crisis — Down to One Nine, No CEO, Losing Ground to Claude Code
*Source: The Pragmatic Engineer (Gergely Orosz)*

GitHub's reliability has collapsed to roughly 90% uptime ("one nine") over the past month, with repeated major outages traced to infrastructure saturation from the explosion of AI agent activity. Claude Code's load on GitHub has 6x'd in three months alone. The root problem: GitHub has no CEO (Microsoft absorbed it into their AI group after Thomas Dohmke left), no clear strategy, and is entangled in internal Microsoft politics. Meanwhile GitHub Copilot — once the leading AI coding tool — has been overtaken by Claude Code and is about to be surpassed by Cursor. A startup called Pierre Computer claims to have already handled 15,000 repo creations per minute (vs GitHub's ~230), positioning itself as an "AI-native" alternative. Pragmatic Engineer contributor Gergely Orosz argues GitHub needs a North Star around agentic infrastructure — and fast. This is a significant story for anyone building on GitHub as core infrastructure.

### 4. Build Your Own Bloomberg Terminal With AI — A Four-Level Framework
*Source: Every (Brooker Belcourt)*

A former hedge fund analyst walks through four escalating levels of AI-powered investment research, from a basic ChatGPT project to a fully autonomous overnight earnings analysis agent. Level one is a custom GPT project with uploaded SEC filings; level two adds a data connector pulling structured financials directly; level three replaces hours of transcript cross-referencing with one prompt; level four — the "Bloomberg killer" — is a single four-word command that runs an entire investment analyst workflow while you sleep. The article is a useful framework for any knowledge work that's structured and repeatable: the gap between "I tried AI and it failed" and "AI does my job better than I do" is almost always about tooling sophistication, not the underlying technology. Finance teams are learning this faster than most industries.

### 5. Silicon Valley Embraces Industrial Policy — and OpenAI Quietly Kills Sora
*Source: Newcomer (Madeline Renbarger & Tom Dotan)*

Two big stories in one issue. First: the Hill & Valley Forum in Washington revealed that Silicon Valley's free-market ideology has quietly collapsed — Jamie Dimon now endorses industrial policy "reluctantly," and the entire tech establishment has united behind government support for AI data centers and manufacturing as a geopolitical imperative against China. Notably absent: any discussion of the administration's corruption, and any reflection on why AI polls worse than ICE with the American public. Second: OpenAI shut down Sora with less than 24 hours' notice to Disney — which had signed a $1B deal just months ago to integrate Disney characters into the product. The lesson: OpenAI has pivoted hard to enterprise (agentic coding, APIs), and "side quests" like consumer video generation are being jettisoned as waste. The Hollywood-OpenAI relationship is effectively over; Google VEO, Runway ML, and Luma Labs will fill the void.

### 6. Vibe Coding SwiftUI Apps Is Surprisingly Good (and Surprisingly Untrustworthy)
*Source: Simon Willison's Newsletter*

Simon Willison built two full macOS menu-bar monitoring apps — Bandwidther (network bandwidth by process) and Gpuer (GPU/memory usage) — using Claude Opus 4.6 and GPT-5.4 with minimal SwiftUI knowledge and without opening Xcode once. A single text file per app (880–1063 lines), built in hours through natural language prompts. The key insight: Claude has surprisingly good design taste in SwiftUI, and "having Claude suggest features is better than having me suggest them because it has a better idea of what's possible." However, Willison adds an important caveat: vibe-coded apps that measure system internals are inherently untrustworthy — Claude got a memory reading wrong this morning, and he can't evaluate whether the numbers are correct because he doesn't know enough about macOS internals. He filed both repos with prominent warnings. The gap between "builds fast" and "builds correctly" remains the core challenge of vibe coding.
