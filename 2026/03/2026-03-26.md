### 1. How Anthropic's Claude Thinks — ByteByteGo
Anthropic's interpretability team built a "microscope" for Claude — a replacement model that swaps neurons for interpretable "features" so researchers can trace and intervene in actual computations. The findings are striking: Claude operates in an abstract, language-independent concept space (French and English questions trigger identical internal features, translated only at output time). When writing poetry, it picks the rhyme-word destination *before* writing the line — the opposite of what researchers expected. For arithmetic, it runs two parallel strategies (rough estimate + last-digit calculation) rather than the carrying algorithm it describes when asked. On hard problems, its chain-of-thought can be a plausible *post-hoc reconstruction* rather than a faithful trace of its work. Hallucinations happen when a "known entity" feature misfires and suppresses Claude's default "I can't answer" circuit. And grammar-coherence features can temporarily override safety features mid-sentence — which is why certain acrostic jailbreaks work. These results hold only for ~25% of tested prompts and are studied on a copy model, not Claude directly.
*Source: ByteByteGo — [Read more](https://blog.bytebytego.com/p/how-anthropics-claude-thinks)*

### 2. Measuring AI Impact Without Overclaiming Causation — Engineering Enablement
A common ROI measurement mistake: comparing developers who use AI heavily to those who don't, then attributing the higher throughput to AI — but heavy AI users were already the most productive. Longitudinal analysis is better but trickier: you must control for management pressure to increase output simultaneously (a classic Goodhart's Law problem). Abi Noda proposes a two-bucket ROI framework: **Amplification** (how much more productive are humans — throughput, time saved, developer experience scores) and **Augmentation** (how much are AI agents extending capacity, measured in "human-equivalent hours" divided by cost = agent hourly rate). For metrics, PR throughput beats lines of code as a signal because LOC is noisier than ever with AI-generated code. DX's "TrueThroughput" uses LOC as one weighted input among several, which large tech companies have also converged on.

### 3. Twitching Before You Sprint — Fish Food for Thought
Drawing on a 2013 neuroscience paper about sleeping rats, Mike Fisher argues that organisms (and organizations) need to "twitch" — make small, low-stakes, self-initiated movements — before being asked to perform at full load. When rats sleep, tiny twitches help the brain build an internal body map; companies that skip this phase commit to scale before understanding their own joints. Applied to product development, "twitches" are prototypes, feature flags, and concierge MVPs — not tools for speed, but expressions of humility that keep the product light enough to learn. In org design, temporary operating models and time-boxed governance experiments reveal where friction actually lives (which rarely matches the org chart). Strategically, small parallel bets let organizations stay coherent long enough for signals to emerge. The leadership posture: admitting uncertainty earns more trust than forced confidence, and leaders who allow twitching avoid the painful pendulum swings that come from skipping the learning phase.
*Source: Fish Food for Thought — [Read more](https://mikefisher.substack.com/p/twitching-before-you-sprint)*

### 4. Instagram's Cofounder on Why Great Products Are Still Hard to Build — Every
In a Context Window interview, Instagram's cofounder pushes back on the idea that AI tools make product-building easy. Technical skills remain essential even in the vibe-coding era: judgment about what to build, taste in what makes something great, and the ability to diagnose when AI-generated code is wrong all require real expertise. The floor has dropped (more people can build something), but the ceiling for great products hasn't moved — and the gap between "working prototype" and "great product" may actually be harder to close when AI writes code you don't fully understand.
*Source: Every — [Read more](https://every.to/context-window/instagram-s-cofounder-on-why-great-products-are-still-hard-to-build)*

### 5. You Will Know Nothing And Be Happy — SeattleDataGuy
A cautionary essay imagining 2030 where developers no longer understand the codebases they "maintain." The author compares AI-assisted coding without comprehension to GPS eroding navigational memory — we're flipping to the back of the math book and assuming we understand the solution. Symptoms visible today: more SEVs, longer debugging cycles, seniors pulled in to validate what AI shipped, "just rewrite it" replacing root-cause analysis. The author isn't anti-AI — he draws a sharp line between using AI to *accelerate* thinking and using AI to *replace* thinking. Without mental maps of your codebase, debugging is guesswork and architecture decisions are noise.
*Source: SeattleDataGuy — [Read more](https://seattledataguy.substack.com/p/you-will-know-nothing-and-be-happy)*
