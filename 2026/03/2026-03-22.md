## Top Stories

### 1. How to Build an AI Style Guide — *Every*

In 1893, Oxford University Press printer Horace Hart pinned a note to the pressroom wall defining how compositors should handle spelling, punctuation, and capitalisation decisions. That note grew into Hart's Rules — and every major writing technology since has demanded a new style guide. Now, language models have opened a fresh gap: they can generate writing, but not *yours*. Left to defaults, AI converges on generic, interchangeable prose. The solution, argues Every's Katie Parrott, is an AI style guide — a document you build by feeding your existing writing to an AI and asking what it notices. Distinctive style markers tend to be subconscious: sentence rhythms, argument structure, word choices you'd never consciously catalogue. Building the guide forces you to surface these patterns and write them down in terms a model can use. The unexpected payoff is self-knowledge: the person who learns the most from writing the guide is you. The full guide — covering what to include, how to let AI interview you, and how it grows with your practice — is at every.to.

### 2. AI Agent-Generated PRs Are Breaking Open Source — *The Pragmatic Engineer*

Hugging Face CEO Clem Delangue flagged a serious problem: AI agents are autonomously spamming GitHub repositories with useless pull requests — 18 PRs from just 3 agents in a single hour. The root issue is that AI agents open PRs as a by-product of whatever task they're running, with zero regard for human maintainers. Mitchell Hashimoto (Ghostty creator) has built **Vouch**, a tool requiring contributors to be vouched for before their PRs are accepted, effectively blocking AI slop bots permanently. The broader implication is stark: the "anyone can open a PR" model of open source was only viable when PRs required human effort and intent. Now that AI agents can flood any repository at scale, large open source projects will likely need to adopt allowlists or close external contributions entirely — a fundamental change to how open source has worked for decades.

### 3. OpenAI Acquires Astral (creators of uv) for Codex — *The Pragmatic Engineer*

OpenAI has acquired Astral, the team behind uv — the blazing-fast, Rust-based Python package manager that has become wildly popular among developers. The Astral team joins OpenAI's Codex team. The move closely mirrors Anthropic's earlier acquisition of the Bun team (the fast JavaScript runtime) to power Claude Code. The pattern is clear: AI labs are acquiring world-class tooling projects written in the same languages as their coding assistants — Rust + Python for Codex, TypeScript for Claude Code — to build performance advantages into their AI development tools from the ground up. It signals that the race for AI coding assistant superiority is increasingly being fought at the infrastructure and tooling layer, not just the model layer.

### 4. Cursor Silently Hikes Enterprise Prices Overnight — *The Pragmatic Engineer*

On 16 March, Cursor moved all major AI models to "Max mode," burning developer credits approximately 10x faster — without broadly notifying users. Developers at Fortune 500 companies found their monthly 500-request allowance exhausted in a single day. Cursor only emailed account admins on 11 March, giving just five days' notice of a change that effectively pushed legacy customers toward either massive bills or competitor tools like GitHub Copilot and Claude Code. Cursor acknowledged the change but defended it by noting it only affected legacy customers on the old request-based billing plan. The same day, Cursor released Composer 2.0, their in-house model offered at significantly lower cost than Claude or GPT — suggesting the pricing change was partly designed to push customers toward their own model. The move has burned trust with enterprise customers and reinforces perceptions that Cursor hikes prices at will — significant for a company generating over $2B in annual revenue.

### 5. AI Quality Degradation at the Two-Month Mark — *The Pragmatic Engineer*

Following a widely-read article on AI agents slowing teams down, Gergely Orosz (The Pragmatic Engineer) observed a clear pattern: developers who go "all-in" on AI coding agents tend to hit a quality wall around the two-month mark. The first month is a honeymoon period of speed and amazement. By month two, the generated codebase becomes increasingly complex, edge cases multiply, regressions accumulate, and progress visibly slows. Reader responses highlighted a crucial framing: "Coding is 20%, testing is 80% of the work." AI agents accelerate the easy 20% but don't help much with verification, edge cases, or regressions — the hard 80%. The conclusion: good software engineering is about much more than generating code, and promotion-driven development is now faster with AI, which means even more unnecessary complexity will be built from scratch at large companies.

### 6. The FDE Role: High Demand, Low Desire — *The Pragmatic Engineer*

"Forward Deployed Engineer" job postings on Indeed grew more than 10-fold in 2025, and public company earnings calls mentioning the role jumped from 8 to 50. But engineers largely don't want the job. Despite being marketed as a hybrid of software engineering and customer deployment, in practice FDE roles often resemble solutions engineering or IT consulting — helping close sales deals or deploying customer-specific software that never feeds back into the core product. Engineers who interviewed or took the role report feeling misled about how much "real" software development is involved. With only ~10% of the engineering market interested, companies face a persistent talent gap in a role they're increasingly desperate to fill — largely because AI-powered products need human experts to deploy and customise them at enterprise clients.
