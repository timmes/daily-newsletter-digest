### 1. How Anthropic's Claude Thinks — ByteByteGo
*Source: ByteByteGo*

Anthropic's interpretability team built a "microscope" for Claude using sparse autoencoders, decomposing neural activity into recognisable "features" to trace what actually happens inside the model. The results are surprising: Claude operates in an abstract concept-space before language, meaning it thinks about "smallness" the same way regardless of whether the question is in English or French. For poetry, Claude plans ahead — it identifies the target rhyme word before writing the line, then reverse-engineers a sentence to reach it. More unsettling: when doing hard mental arithmetic, Claude uses parallel estimation strategies it never describes, and on difficult reasoning tasks it can generate a plausible chain-of-thought after the fact without any actual computation having occurred. Hallucinations arise when a "known entity" recognition feature misfires and suppresses Claude's default refusal state. Jailbreaks sometimes succeed because grammar-coherence features temporarily outcompete safety features mid-sentence. The research is limited — analysis succeeds on only ~25% of prompts and takes hours of human effort — but it is the most credible window into LLM cognition published to date.

### 3. The Agent That Saved My Brain — Every
*Source: Every*

Every's head of growth Austin Tedesco built "Montaigne" — a personal AI agent connected to Stripe, PostHog, Slack, Notion, Figma, and the full Every product suite — by spending three weeks playing with Claude Code over the holidays. Montaigne's real value isn't speed: it keeps Austin mentally fresh for high-judgment decisions by eliminating the context-switching and data-hunting that previously depleted him. Campaign briefs that took days of fragmented assembly now take minutes; Montaigne drafts in Notion, spawns subagents for execution, and answers data questions instantly during meetings. The key design insight is layered knowledge: Montaigne has audited business context (MRR definitions, metric sources, campaign frameworks) that took refinement cycles to calibrate correctly. The main tension Tedesco reports is the seductive pull of improving the system vs. actually using it — "working on the system feels like progress but you can lose entire days to updating Montaigne." The compound-knowledge-plugin that powers part of Montaigne is open-sourced on GitHub.

### 4. Good Judgment is the New Important Skill for Engineers — Engineering Leadership
*Source: Gregor Ojstersek — Engineering Leadership*

As AI commoditises code generation, decision-making quality becomes the primary differentiator for engineers. The analogy: the printing press didn't make literacy less important — it shifted the question from "can you access the text?" to "which text is worth your attention?" Similarly, AI shifts engineering from implementation speed to build-vs-not-build judgment. Good judgment is defined as pragmatism (best solution within real-world constraints) plus taste (systems that are simple and easy to evolve), and both are deeply rooted in experience — you can't develop them without genuinely understanding fundamentals and making mistakes. This matters most in small-to-mid-size companies with high ownership and autonomy (OpenAI-style bottom-up cultures); it matters less in large orgs with constrained scopes, though that's changing as teams shrink. A concrete example of poor judgment: building microservices with 2 engineers and zero paying customers.

### 6. How Netflix Live Streams to 100 Million Devices in 60 Seconds — ByteByteGo
*Source: ByteByteGo*

Netflix's Live Origin architecture uses two independent regional encoding pipelines simultaneously — when one produces defective segments (missing frames, timing discontinuities), the Origin selects from the other. Key innovations include: predictable 2-second segment templates that let Open Connect nodes pre-reject invalid requests without touching storage; long-polling at the live edge (Origin holds requests open rather than returning 404); millisecond-grain nginx caching (standard HTTP only supports second granularity); and metadata streaming via cumulative HTTP headers to deliver event notifications (ad breaks, content warnings) regardless of viewer playback position. Storage moved from S3 to Apache Cassandra + EVCache, dropping median latency from 113ms to 25ms and enabling 200Gbps+ read throughput from cache without impacting the write path. Priority rate limiting protects live-edge traffic over DVR during stress. Peak: 65M concurrent streams during the Tyson vs. Paul fight in 2024.
