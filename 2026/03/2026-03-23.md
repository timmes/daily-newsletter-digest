### 1. How to Build an AI Style Guide — Every
*By Katie Parrott | [Read more](https://every.to/guides/ai-style-guide)*

Just as Horace Hart's 1893 printer's rules grew into the 200-page Oxford style manual, AI demands a new kind of style guide — but this time for your personal voice. Language models left to their defaults converge on generic, interchangeable prose, and humans are roughly twice as linguistically varied as machines sentence to sentence. The fix is to feed your own writing to an AI and ask it what patterns it notices, surfacing subconscious habits like argument structure and sentence rhythm that you'd never consciously articulate. Over weeks you build a document of named rules — what to do, what to avoid — that you upload to a Claude Project or custom GPT. The insight is that vague descriptors like "smart" or "conversational" don't help; you need to be specific enough that a machine can follow the rule. Crucially, writing the guide teaches you more about your own voice than it teaches the AI.

### 2. Is the FDE Role Becoming Less Desirable? — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

Forward Deployed Engineer job postings grew more than 10× on Indeed in 2025 vs 2024, yet only about 10% of engineers actually want the role. The gap exists because companies pitch FDE as deep software engineering, while the reality is closer to sales engineer or solutions consultant — deploying a client-specific wrapper on the product team's platform with little back-contribution to the codebase. Engineers who take the role often quit within weeks after realising there's "nothing on software development." In a tight job market, the mismatch between supply and demand for a well-paying role is a reliable signal that something is structurally wrong with the offer itself.

### 3. AI Agent–Generated PRs Are Breaking Open Source — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

Hugging Face's CEO flagged 18 AI-generated pull requests from just 3 agents in a single hour — one PR every three minutes — all noise, zero value. The problem isn't that AI can write code; it's that agents open PRs as a side effect of doing other things, with no regard for human reviewers. Mitchell Hashimoto (HashiCorp founder) built a tool called Vouch that requires new contributors to be vouched for by existing trusted contributors, blocking AI bots from spamming repos. The broader "anyone can open a PR" model of open source — which assumed human effort as a natural rate limiter — may already be functionally dead; most large projects will likely move to allowlists or Vouch-style gates.

### 4. OpenAI Acquires Astral, Creator of uv — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

OpenAI announced the acquisition of Astral, the team behind the Rust-based Python package manager `uv`, whose members will join the Codex team. This mirrors Anthropic's earlier acquisition of the Bun JavaScript runtime team for Claude Code — both AI labs are buying world-class tooling teams whose language (Rust) matches the language of their own coding assistant. The pattern is clear: labs are vertically integrating the performance-critical layers of the developer stack to give their coding tools a speed and reliability edge.

### 5. Cursor Silently Hikes Enterprise Prices — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

On March 16 Cursor switched all major models to "Max mode" for customers on legacy request-based plans, making credits burn 10× faster — without telling developers, only emailing account admins five days in advance. Engineers at Fortune 500 companies exhausted their monthly 500-request budgets in a single day. Cursor admitted the change affected a small subset of legacy users and justified it by releasing their in-house Composer 2.0 model the same day to push users off expensive third-party models (Claude/GPT). The episode reinforces a damaging perception: Cursor hikes prices at will without transparent communication, which is a trust-burning move for a $2B-ARR company that depends on developer loyalty.

### 6. Microsoft May Sue OpenAI and AWS Over $50B Cloud Deal — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

The 2023 Microsoft–OpenAI deal requires all API access to OpenAI models to route through Azure — a condition Microsoft secured as part of a $10B investment that also gives it 49% ownership and up to 75% of OpenAI profits. When OpenAI announced a $50B partnership with AWS to run models on Amazon hardware, Microsoft flagged a contract breach and threatened litigation. OpenAI and Amazon claim they've engineered a workaround; Microsoft's position is blunt: "We know our contract. We will sue them if they breach it." The dispute lays bare how dependent OpenAI became on Microsoft's terms in its early capital-scarce days — terms that now constrain its ability to diversify cloud infrastructure.

### 7. Block's 40% Layoffs Triggered by AI Model Leap — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

Jack Dorsey confirmed in a Wired interview that Block's decision to cut 40% of its workforce began in December 2025 — precisely when Anthropic's Opus 4.6 and OpenAI's Codex 5.3 crossed a threshold from "good at greenfield" to "good at large, existing codebases." Dorsey framed it as a structural rethink: "We have to rethink how companies run, how they're structured, how they're built." This is one of the first named, public examples of a major company explicitly attributing a large-scale layoff to a specific AI capability jump rather than to macro-economic conditions.

### 8. The Two-Month AI Quality Cliff — The Pragmatic Engineer
*By Gergely Orosz | The Pulse*

Reader feedback on last week's "Are AI agents actually slowing us down?" article surfaced a recurring pattern: developers who went "all-in" on Claude Code, Codex, and similar tools around January 2026 are now — roughly two months later — hitting a quality wall. The theory: month one is a honeymoon of speed and volume; by month two, complexity accumulates, regressions pile up, and progress visibly slows. The root cause isn't the tools — it's inadequate planning and design up-front, plus the underestimated reality that "coding is 20%, testing is 80% of the work." AI is great at generating code to spec; it doesn't replace the human judgment needed to define what to build.
