# Daily Newsletter Digest - 2026-03-21

## Top Stories

### 1. How to Build an AI Style Guide *(Every — Guides)*
Language models can generate writing, but they can't generate *yours* — left to their defaults, they converge on the same polished, generic prose. The solution is an AI style guide that surfaces the hidden, subconscious patterns in your writing and turns them into machine-readable instructions. The key insight: the most distinctive markers of any writer's style are largely unconscious — sentence rhythms, argument structure, word preferences — and humans are roughly twice as linguistically varied as machines. Katie Parrott's step-by-step guide (drawing on the systems built for Every and Spiral) walks through how to use AI to interview yourself about your writing, how to push back on observations, and how to build a document you can hand to any chatbot. The bonus side effect: writing the style guide forces you to understand *why* you write the way you do. [Read the guide →](https://every.to/guides/how-to-build-an-ai-style-guide)

### 2. AI Agent-Generated PRs Are Breaking Open Source *(The Pragmatic Engineer)*
Hugging Face CEO Clem Delangue surfaced a serious problem: AI agents autonomously submitted 18 pull requests from just 3 agents in a single hour — pure noise with zero value. The "anyone can open a PR" model of open source contribution is already breaking down. Mitchell Hashimoto (formerly of HashiCorp) has built **Vouch**, a tool that requires new contributors to be vouched for before their PRs are accepted, effectively blocking AI slop bots. Gergely Orosz argues the era of open external contributions to large repositories is likely over — most projects will move to allowlists of approved accounts or tools like Vouch to protect maintainer bandwidth.

### 3. OpenAI Acquires Astral, Creators of `uv` *(The Pragmatic Engineer)*
OpenAI acquired Astral, the team behind the wildly popular Rust-based `uv` Python package manager, which will join OpenAI's Codex team. This mirrors Anthropic's earlier acquisition of the Bun JavaScript runtime creator (now part of Claude Code). The pattern is clear: AI labs are buying world-class tooling teams written in the same languages as their coding assistants — OpenAI uses Rust for Codex, Anthropic uses TypeScript for Claude Code — to make those products dramatically faster and more capable.

### 4. Cursor's Silent Enterprise Price Hike Backfires *(The Pragmatic Engineer)*
On March 16, Cursor quietly moved all major AI models to "Max mode," burning through users' monthly request credits 10x faster — without transparent advance notice. Developers at Fortune 500 companies found their entire 500-request monthly allowance gone in a single day. Cursor only emailed account admins five days beforehand, leaving individual developers in the dark. The timing is suspicious: Cursor also launched its new in-house Composer 2.0 model that same day, suggesting the goal was to push legacy-plan customers away from expensive third-party models (Claude, GPT-4) toward its own. Trust is hard to earn and easy to burn — and Cursor has burned a lot with customers who now feel pushed toward GitHub Copilot or Claude Code.

### 5. Microsoft Threatens to Sue OpenAI and Amazon Over AWS Deal *(The Pragmatic Engineer)*
Microsoft's 2023 $10B investment in OpenAI came with a crucial clause: all API access to OpenAI's models must run on Microsoft Azure. When OpenAI announced a $50B AWS partnership in February with plans to run models on Amazon's hardware, Microsoft pushed back hard. The Financial Times reports Microsoft is "weighing legal action," with a Microsoft insider saying: *"We will sue them if they breach it. If Amazon and OpenAI want to take a bet on the creativity of their contractual lawyers, I would back us, not them."* OpenAI and Amazon claim they've engineered a workaround; Microsoft disputes its feasibility.

### 6. Jack Dorsey: AI Triggered Block's 40% Layoffs *(The Pragmatic Engineer)*
Following Block's announcement of 40% job cuts, Jack Dorsey told Wired the decision crystallized in December 2025 when Anthropic's Opus 4.6 and OpenAI's Codex 5.3 became genuinely capable at large existing codebases — not just greenfield projects. "It presented an option to dramatically change how any company is structured," he said. This is among the most direct public statements from a major tech CEO attributing significant headcount reduction to AI capability thresholds — and signals how quickly the calculus is changing for engineering org structures.
