## 1. Is the FDE role becoming less desirable?

An interesting trend highlighted [by The Wall Street Journal](https://substack.com/redirect/993bf5ae-e85e-45bb-975b-53057e43a393?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI): companies want to hire for FDE roles, but devs are just not that interested:

> “Job postings on Indeed grew more than 10-fold in 2025 compared with 2024. The number of public company transcripts mentioning the role jumped to 50 from eight over the same period, according to data from AlphaSense.
>
>
>
> The only problem? Few engineers want the job, which has historically been seen as demanding, undesirable, and less prestigious than product-focused engineering roles.
>
>
>
> “Everyone wants them and there’s only maybe 10% of the market that wants that role,” said Patrick Kellenberger, president and chief operating officer at Betts Recruiting.“

Last summer, we covered [the rise of the FDE role](https://substack.com/redirect/204e7a1f-29cf-4905-909b-b648fb59ef65?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI), and looked into what it’s like. Back then, this is how I visualized what was then a very hot role:

*Reality of the FDE role: less software engineering, and even less platform engineering*

The role seems akin to a “sales engineer” where FDEs help close the deals, or a solutions engineer (or even consultant), where FDEs deploy to a customer to build them a solution. They don’t contribute back into the platform, and don’t do much that’s considered “software engineering” beyond integrating software which the product team built.

Some engineers figure out the nature of the role during the interview process and pass on it. Meanwhile, some others take the job and later quit. Here’s what a dev told me who accept an FDE role at a company, but didn’t find what they expected:

> “This FDE job was a typical IT services mindset. The company wanted to use me more on the engagement lead side, and nothing on software development. It’s not what I signed up for, and I didn’t like the vibe and culture. I quit 4 weeks later.”

In today’s job market, if there’s high demand for a role which pays decently but attracts little interest from engineers, there’s always a reason!

## 2. AI agent-generated pull requests: a big problem for large open source projects

Hugging Face is the “GitHub for AI and ML,” and a team that maintains several large repositories on GitHub. Cofounder and CEO, Clem Delangue, shared a massive problem: some AI agents are absolutely spamming their repositories with pull requests. Below are 18 pull requests generated by 3 AI agents in just an hour (an average of one new PR every 3 minutes):

| --- | --- | --- |

*Slop pull requests generated by AI agents. Source: [Clem Delangue](https://substack.com/redirect/22cdb292-ac6b-49c0-a90f-c811a5f51bf7?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI)*

The problem is not AI generation, it’s that AI agents keep creating them as part of doing whatever they’re doing at the time. The PRs are useless and pure noise. And the more OpenClaw-like agents there are running, the more “slop” requests are generated.

It’s the same issue that Mitchell Hashimoto discussed on [The Pragmatic Engineer podcast:](https://substack.com/redirect/d92cf8cd-edd1-463b-96f0-0c90eccd0dc5?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI)

> “At a certain point, AI agents started opening PRs autonomously. There was a dead giveaway of AI because – at least to this day when we are recording – the way Claude opens a PR is it opens a draft with no buddy, and then it edits the body, and then it reopens it for review, and it’s happening at a speed that is unrealistic. These AI pull requests are already happening three times a day.
>
>
>
> I wish that these agentic tools would put a pause in opening PRs, because it’s really causing a lot of friction.”

Mitchell has since built [Vouch](https://substack.com/redirect/f68fec69-bf4e-432e-b9f5-0d94a3d05fc5?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI), which is a way to stop AI slop requests. With Vouch, a new contributor needs to be *vouched for*in orderto allow contribution. AI bots generating slop will not be vouched for, and will be denounced from the project and permanently blocked from contributing.

I suspect the “anyone can open a pull request on GitHub” model is already dead, but we just don’t know it yet. It only works until a *human* opens pull requests: for a developer, it takes time and effort to open a PR, and most PRs have thought and intent put into them. In contrast, AI agents open them with ease and with zero regard for a human reviewer. This will likely cause most large open source projects to adopt tools like Vouch, or to simply close external contributions not from an allowlist of approved accounts.

## 3. Industry Pulse

### OpenAI acquires Astral, the creators of uv

Today (Thursday), OpenAI announced that it has [acquired](https://substack.com/redirect/16eb99e6-ced0-480c-8ba7-8ad4945a628f?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI) Astral, the team behind the wildly popular uv package manager, which is very performant and written in Rust. The Astral team will join OpenAI’s Codex team.

This deal almost exactly mirrors the way Anthropic [acquired Bun](https://substack.com/redirect/4ebbe155-6713-4a85-bee4-03762125c7ee?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI), the creator of the blazing JavaScript runtime called Bun. That team joined Claude Code to make it more performant.

Meanwhile, OpenAI’s Codex is written in Rust (as covered in the deepdive, [How Codex is built](https://substack.com/redirect/1334e152-6719-4703-a402-8a502dcac8c7?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI)), and so is uv. It seems like the Codex team is bringing in Rust + Python expertise to make Codex even more performant, in the same way that Claude Code is built in TypeScript, so they invested in a team that’s world-class in TypeScript.

It’s interesting that AI labs are acquiring some of the most performant projects written in the programming languages that they develop their own coding assistant in!

### Cursor suddenly hikes prices for some enterprise customers

A few devs at Fortune 500 companies messaged me earlier this week, very upset about a silent change made by Cursor.

Until 16 March, devs could choose their preferred model (usually ones like Opus) in Cursor, and had 500 agentic requests per month. But Cursor has now moved all major models to “Max mode” that uses up monthly credits easily 10x as fast, without telling anyone! A dev explained the impact:

> “Before the change, you could use any of the latest models, and it would take a fixed amount of requests. My 500 requests would last most of the month if you managed things, and after the 500 requests, we could pay per token. As of March 16th, Cursor is forcing Max Mode on all of the latest models for customers still on the request plan. This means that within a day, you can now exhaust your entire 500 requests that would normally take most of the month to burn through.”

So, devs are running out of Cursor credits and risk either running up a very expensive bill – or being forced by Cursor to use another tool like GitHub Copilot or Claude Code. Devs whom I talked to told me that their org instructed them to move over to one of these tools for development.

I talked with Cursor about the change, and the company admitted they did not widely communicate this pricing change because it only affected a small subset of users on legacy plans: ones who were billed based on requests made, and not tokens used. Cursor only emailed account admins, meaning devs at large companies were in the dark about the change. Even worse, they sent this on 11 March, giving only five days of heads-up about a very significant change.

On one hand, I’m sympathetic to why Cursor needed to make this change: they were probably losing money on legacy customers under the old pricing structure. But it’s not good to do this without open, transparent comms, and by rolling out a major change practically overnight in a way that potentially pushes customers to rival products.

Trust is hard to earn and easy to burn: and Cursor has burnt lots of trust with this move, which reinforces the perception that it hikes prices at will, without communicating or giving reasonable notice. That seems pretty immature by a company considered a leader in AI-assisted development which generates more than $2B in annual revenue.

One reason why Cursor might have rushed with this change is because today (on 19 March) they released their new in-house model called [Composer 2.0](https://substack.com/redirect/a0ebb91d-8c83-4b82-8a7b-9d9cbaddf4ac?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI). Cursor says this model is on par with state-of-the-art coding models, and they are offering it at much lower prices than the Claude or GPT models. Disincentivizing legacy customers from using non-Composer models might have been one goal of Cursor’s team. If only they communicated about it!

### AWS ends support for PGSQL 13 – but doesn’t yet fully support 14

AWS is known for reliability: when services are retired there are clear migration paths, and AWS gives ample warning… usually. In that light, AWS expert Corey Quinn has quite the [story to tell:](https://substack.com/redirect/ced56cb0-4ec9-4f4d-87fb-e35a7b322116?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI)

> “AWS has ended support for PostgreSQL 13 on RDS [Relational Database Service – AWS’s managed database service where they run PostgreSQL for you] and is telling everyone to upgrade immediately [to 14, the next version].
>
>
>
> Great advice! Except for the part where upgrading to PostgreSQL 14 breaks AWS Glue, their own managed ETL service [the Extract, Transform, Load service that pulls data from a place like AWS RDS, and into data warehouses, which is a common thing to do], because the Glue team apparently never got around to supporting the authentication scheme that PostgreSQL 14 shipped with.
>
>
>
> In 2021.
>
>
>
> So your choices are: stay on an unsupported database version, or upgrade and watch AWS’s own services fail to connect to it. It’s a choose-your-own-adventure book where every page says “call support.”

There are solutions to this problem, but they’re “icky.” The options, as [outlined by Corey](https://substack.com/redirect/d2575b4c-02a2-4ae9-bee5-3986d76fdbd8?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI):

> “You need a fix. AWS has provided three of them, and they all suck.
>
>
>
> You can downgrade password encryption on your database to the older, less secure standard: the one you just upgraded away from, per AWS’s own recommendations.
>
>
>
> You can bring your own JDBC driver, which disables connection testing and may not support all the features you want.
>
>
>
> Or you can rewrite your ETL workflows as Python shell jobs.”

The problem seems to be the RDS and Glue teams having different priorities, and not being aware of what the other was doing, or needed doing. The result is a subpar service where customers need to do weird workarounds because one team didn’t support the 2021 PostgreSQL 14 authentication scheme.

### Hang on, how can OpenAI offer its models on AWS?

>

## 4. Re: “Are AI agents actually slowing us down?”

Thanks for all the comments and emails about Tuesday’s article, [Are AI agents actually slowing us down?](https://substack.com/redirect/e5a5ad97-102c-4956-a5ff-99077dfe786e?j=eyJ1IjoiMnZudW4xIn0.SRKHrZ3b3v0LIhYNTUKVt6T3jSk83Z1jDLEzct68YEI) It clearly hit a nerve, and many of you report seeing quality problems from using (or overusing) AI agents.

A few thoughts:

**“Coding is 20%, testing is 80% of the work.”**This line resonated with many readers. As engineers, we still seem to overestimate the importance of *writing* code, even though we may rarely spend most time coding! It was always about the edge cases, verification, and finding and dealing with regressions.

**The two-month mark of being “all-in” on AI might be when quality issues start to show.**I detected an uptick in devs moving over to using Claude Code, Codex, and other AI agentic coding tools, in around January 2026, with many devs starting to generate most of their code with these tools, and generating a lot more of it. Around a fortnight ago, I noted a spike in complaints about quality around AI-generated code.

I wonder if there’s a 1-2 month lag in seeing quality degradations from using these tools. The first month could be a “honeymoon period” of amazement about how much faster coding tasks can be done, and how much more can be generated. Then quality starts to slip, initially in hard-to-spot ways. By the end of month two, the issues are piling up, the code gets way more complex, and progress visibly slows down.

**“It’s not Claude’s fault you don’t do adequate planning or design.”**This comment by regular reader, Hillary, had me nodding in agreement. AI agents are tools that do well with well-defined specifications, but it takes time and effort to decide what to build and how it should work.

I sense that predictions about the death of PMs due to AI and the demise of software engineering from AI are way off because good software engineering is about so much more than merely generating code.
